The Sanskrit textual tradition is replete with descriptions of extraordinary powers (*vibhūti*, *siddhi*) attainable through yoga practice: the ability to become invisible, to fly, to know others' thoughts, to resist disease and death. These descriptions raise interesting questions for the historian of religion and medicine.

How should scholars respond to claims of yogic powers? This post reviews a range of scholarly positions — from dismissal as superstition, through sociological bracketing, to serious engagement with the inner logic of these traditions — and argues that careful attention to the textual and historical contexts in which such claims appear is more productive than either credulity or condescension.
