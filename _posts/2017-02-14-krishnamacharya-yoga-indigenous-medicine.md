In 1946, on the eve of full independence from Britain, the Government of India announced the establishment of a committee to recommend steps to improve facilities for research and training in indigenous systems of medicine and to increase their usefulness to the public.

Tirumalai Krishnamacharya — now widely recognised as one of the most influential figures in twentieth-century yoga — positioned yoga squarely within this discourse of indigenous medicine. This post examines how Krishnamacharya articulated the relationship between yoga and āyurveda and what that articulation tells us about the politics of knowledge in late-colonial and early post-independence India.
